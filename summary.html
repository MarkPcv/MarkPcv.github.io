Design Summary


To create an effective and autonomous robot, different subsystems of the robot were made to utilise the various sensors and hardware components made available. Armed with the following, H.A.R.M. integrates sensory input to provide intelligent actuation and perform autonomous navigation, collision avoidance and fire extinguishing.






Hardware Components



Mecanum Wheels


With multiple free-moving rollers attached at 45° angles, Mecanum wheels are an effective means for achieving omnidirectional motion. By controlling the output to each wheel forwards, backwards, rotational and strafing movements can be achieved.


Arduino board


The brains of the operation, the Arduino takes measurements from the connected sensors and uses a finite state machine and behavioural control logic to identify the correct course of action.


Motion Processing Unit (MPU9250)


A 9DOF inertial measurement unit that consists of a 3-axis gyroscope, 3-axis accelerometer, 3-axis magnetometer and an on-board digital motion processor (DMP). Sensor fusion is used to combine measurements and continuously provide H.A.R.Mï¿½s heading/yaw angle in the arena for localisation making turns.


Electric Fan


H.A.R.M. keeps his cool when placed in front of a fire by activating a 5V DC electric fan connected to one of the Arduino's digital output pins. Placed in a custom 3D printed bracket, the fan is placed at an optimal height and kept on until the fire has been eradicated.


Phototransistors


Capable of detecting close light sources, 2 large phototransistors and 2 small phototransistors are used to determine if any fires are present in close proximity to the robot. They are the fire-fighting eyes of the robot.


Proximity Sensors: SONAR + Infrared


Tested at a range of distances, H.A.R.M.'s non-contact, ultrasonic Sonar sensor was found to have an accurate working range between approximately 7cm and 80cm. The short range IR sensors were found to produce reliable readings between 5-30cm and for the medium range, 5-80cm. These proximity sensors form the eyes of the robot and allow obstacle identification and distinguishing.


Servo


Capable of rotating 180°, the servo is controlled using PWM. Mounting the phototransistors and fan on the servo allows H.A.R.M. to scan his environment and catch any wild-fire situations that require immediate assistance. Control to the servo is adjusted when a light source is detected so that continuous fire tracking can take place.




Physical Design


Several proximity sensors are placed at the front to enable detection and distinguishing of obstacles and walls while two are also placed on the left side so that H.A.R.M. is able to align himself to the wall and reestablish his position.
